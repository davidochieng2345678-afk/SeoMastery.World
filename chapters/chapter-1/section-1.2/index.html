Section 1.2: How Search Engines Actually Work

By David Kiruo | Nairobi, Kenya — For the World

When I first started learning SEO, I treated Google like a magic box. I put content in, hoped rankings came out, and had absolutely no idea what was happening in between. That was my first and biggest mistake.

Because once you understand how Google actually works, every SEO decision you make becomes logical — not guesswork. You stop hoping and start knowing.

Think of Google as the world's most hardworking librarian. Except instead of managing one library, this librarian manages over 130 trillion web pages and has to answer over 8.5 billion questions every single day — and answer them correctly, instantly, in the right language, for the right location.

The Three Processes of Google

To do this impossible job, Google uses three processes that run continuously, every second of every day. Understanding these three processes is the foundation of everything in this book.

1

🕷️ Crawling

Google sends out tiny software robots called Crawlers or Spiders. They travel across the entire internet, clicking every link they find, reading every page, and reporting back to Google.

2

📚 Indexing

After crawling a page, Google stores the information in its massive database called the Index. Think of it as Google's giant library catalogue.

3

🏆 Ranking

When someone searches, Google scans its entire index and ranks the most relevant, trustworthy, and helpful pages at the top.

🇰🇪 Simple Analogy — The Nairobi Library

Imagine the Nairobi City Library. The librarian sends out a scout (crawling) who walks through the city finding new books. Each book is recorded, categorised, and filed in the right section (indexing). When you ask for a book on "Kenyan history," the librarian finds the most relevant, most trusted book and hands it to you first (ranking).

Google does exactly this — with 130 trillion pages, in milliseconds.

Process 1: Crawling — How Google Finds You

Google employs software robots called Googlebots or Spiders. Right now, as you read this, millions of these bots are crawling the internet — visiting pages, reading content, following links, and reporting everything back to Google's servers.

Think of each link on the internet as a road. Googlebot starts on a known road, drives to a page, reads everything on it, then follows every link on that page to new roads, and so on — forever. This is why links are so powerful in SEO. More links pointing to your site means more roads leading to Google finding you.

What Stops Google From Crawling Your Site?

  • Blocking Googlebot — Accidentally blocking Google in your robots.txt file
  • Login walls — Pages hidden behind passwords or login forms
  • Broken links — Internal links leading to dead pages (404 errors)
  • Slow speed — Very slow page speed makes bots give up and leave
  • No sitemap — Missing sitemap submitted to Google Search Console
  • JavaScript issues — Heavy JavaScript that bots cannot read properly
How to check if Google can crawl your site:
→ Go to Google Search Console
→ Click "URL Inspection"
→ Enter your page URL
→ See "Crawl allowed? Yes/No"

Process 2: Indexing — How Google Stores You

After Googlebot crawls your page, it sends the information back to Google's servers where it is analysed, understood, and stored in Google's massive database — called the Index.

Here is the hard truth: If your page is not in the index, it does not exist as far as Google is concerned. It will never appear in search results. Period.

What Google Stores for Each Page

  • The Content: Every word, heading, and paragraph on your page
  • The Links: Who links to you and who you link to
  • Freshness: When the page was published and last updated
  • Mobile Experience: How the page looks and works on a phone
  • Page Speed: How fast the page loads for real users
  • Security: Whether the site uses HTTPS encryption

🛠️ How to Check if Google Has Indexed Your Site

Open Google and type this into the search bar — replace with your domain:

site:yourdomain.com

If you see your pages, you are indexed. If you see nothing, Google cannot find you. To fix this, submit your sitemap through Google Search Console — we cover this fully in Chapter 3.

Process 3: Ranking — How Google Decides Who Wins

This is the part everyone wants to know. Crawling and indexing get your pages into Google. Ranking determines where they appear.

Ranking is decided by Google's algorithm — a complex system of over 200 ranking factors that score every page in the index and arrange them in order of relevance and quality.

The Most Important Ranking Factors

  • Content Quality & Relevance (Very High Impact) — Does your content actually answer the searcher's question better than anyone else?
  • Backlinks (Very High Impact) — Links from other trusted websites are like votes of confidence. This is still Google's #1 ranking factor.
  • Mobile Friendliness (High Impact) — Over 80% of people browse on mobile. Google ranks the mobile version of your site first.
  • Page Speed (High Impact) — If your site takes more than 3 seconds to load, you lose both rankings and visitors.
  • Keyword Optimisation (High Impact) — Using the right words in the right places signals what your content is about.
  • User Experience Signals (Medium Impact) — How long do visitors stay? Do they click back immediately? Google watches.
🔑

David's Key Takeaway

Crawling gets you found. Indexing gets you stored. Ranking gets you seen. All three must work together. Before you do any SEO work, check these three things first: Can Google crawl you? Are you indexed? What are you currently ranking for? Use Google Search Console — it is completely free and answers all three questions in minutes.
